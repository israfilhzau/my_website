About Me

I am currently pursuing a PhD in Plant Nutrition (Soil Science) from the University of China Academy of Science (UCAS) China, MS in Food Science and Technology (Microbiology), and a Bachelor of Science in Agriculture. My research centers on the intricate relationships between soil health, plant nutrition, and microbial interactions, aiming to develop innovative solutions to enhance crop productivity and promote food security on a global scale.

With a deep interest in bioinformatics and molecular biology, I utilize cutting-edge technologies to investigate genomic, transcriptomic, and microbial data, driving insights that inform more sustainable agricultural systems. By combining my interdisciplinary expertise with rigorous experimentation, I strive to contribute valuable knowledge that can transform agricultural practices and improve global food production, all while minimizing environmental impact.

Master Thesis Abstract

Food safety, a global priority, is based on reducing the ongoing risk of food-borne pathogens. Among these, Salmonella stands out as one of the major adversaries, continuously demanding attention from food science researchers. Its impact resonates globally, causing significant illness and imposing an economic burden. This challenge intensifies with the emergence of antibiotic and disinfectant-resistant strains, highlighting the urgent need for novel control strategies. Phages are naturally occurring bacterial predators with immense potential. While their efficacy against bacteria like Salmonella is promising, understanding their interactions remains limited. This thesis delves into this gap by comprehensively characterizing three Salmonella phages through phenotypic and genomic analysis, also elucidating their interactions with various Salmonella host strains. The following aspects were studied in this thesis.

Phage screening for biocontrol potential: In order to explore the potential of phages to combat Salmonella contamination in food safety settings, 190 laboratory-preserved Salmonella phages were first screened for host range and lytic activity against diverse Salmonella strains using phage typing methods. Initially, a rigorous screening process was conducted to evaluate the lytic performance and host range of three phages named LPSP56, LPST100, and LPSC20, respectively.

Biological characterization of potent phages: Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) imaging revealed distinct morphological features for each phage. Analysis of their one-step growth kinetics indicated that LPSP56 and LPSC20 had latent periods of 20 minutes, while LPST100 required 40 minutes. The burst sizes were determined to be 220±20 PFU/cell for LPSP56, 420±20 PFU/cell for LPST100, and 150±30 PFU/cell for LPSC20. These findings collectively suggested a short incubation period coupled with high lytic potential for all three phages. Additionally, the phages exhibited stability across a wide range of pH values (4-11) and temperatures (30°C-60°C), indicating their potential adaptability to various food storage or processing conditions.

The bactericidal effects of a phage cocktail on Salmonella in food: The phage cocktail exhibited remarkable efficacy in reducing Salmonella levels in chicken meat and lettuce, with significant decreases observed at the multiplicity of infection (MOI) of 100 and MOI of 1000 within 12 hours of treatment at both 4°C and 25°C. Specifically, the cocktail achieved maximum effectiveness at an MOI of 1000, resulting in 50% and 73% reduction in Salmonella counts on meat and lettuce, respectively. Notably, at 4°C, the phage cocktail demonstrated a pronounced reduction in bacterial counts, indicating its potential as a food safety enhancer. These findings underscore the potential of phage cocktails in effectively controlling zoonotic Salmonella in food systems and their application in food safety measures.

Genomic insights into safe and diverse biocontrol agents: Whole-genome sequencing of the three isolated phages, LPSP56, LPST100, and LPSC20, provided valuable insights into their biocontrol potential. Notably, none of the phages harbored genes associated with virulence, antibiotic resistance, or integration into bacterial hosts, solidifying their safety for biocontrol applications. Cluster analysis of phage species revealed significant diversity among them, highlighting the vast potential in the phage world. This diversity is further supported by whole-genome sequencing, which identified LPSP56 and LPSC20 as belonging to the Guernseyvirinae subfamily and LPST100 to the Ackermannviridae family. To further classify these phages, a phylogenetic tree was constructed based on the major capsid protein (MCP) sequences. By comparing homologous proteins with a similarity threshold of over 40% and utilizing the intergenomic distance calculator (VIRIDIC), LPSP56 and LPST100 were identified as novel phage species belonging to the Jerseyvirus and Kuttervirus genera, respectively.

This study successfully identified and characterized three promising phages (LPSP56, LPST100, and LPSC20) with significant potential for controlling Salmonella contamination in food. These phages exhibited broad host range, strong lytic activity, good thermal and pH stability, and efficient reduction of Salmonella levels in chicken meat and lettuce. Furthermore, genomic analysis confirmed their safety for biocontrol applications and revealed their remarkable diversity, including the discovery of two novel phage species. These findings pave the way for the development of novel and safe phage-based interventions to enhance food safety and combat the potential threat of zoonotic Salmonella pathogens.

Keywords: Zoonotic Salmonella, Phages, Phage Cocktail, Host spectrum, Food safety.
